Directly comparing the number of EV charging stations to gas stations doesn't give an accurate picture of how available each type of infrastructure actually is. San Diego data shows around 3,000 public EV chargers and about 900 gas stations, but that comparison is misleading on its own. At a basic level, one charging station can only charge one EV at a time, while one gas station can fuel several vehicles at once. A better comparison is EV chargers to gas pumps. At a conservative estimate of 6 pumps per station, that puts the total number of gas pumps at around 5,400. Even with this low estimate, based on each station having just 3 pumps on each side, the two numbers end up fairly close. Given that EVs make up a much smaller share of the vehicle market, shouldn't that closeness raise the question of whether EVs are getting too much investment?
The time it takes to fully refuel an EV versus a standard car is wildly different, and that gap drastically reduces the total number of cars each type of station can serve per day. Gas pumps typically take around 3 minutes per car, ignoring other factors, while EV charging time depends heavily on the charger and station type. Charging speed comes down to the charger's level: Level 1, Level 2, or Level 3 (DC fast charging). Level 1 chargers are for home use, charging an EV overnight over 12+ hours. These are considered private, so they aren't included in the 3,000 figure. Level 2 and Level 3 chargers are generally the public ones, with an 80% charge taking 4+ hours or about 30 minutes, respectively. San Diego has around 300 public Level 2 and 3 chargers total, but even if every public charger was Level 3 (an impossible assumption), they'd still only fuel EVs at roughly 1/10th the rate of gas pumps. That means gas pumps effectively have 10 times the throughput once you factor in fueling speed.
The last factor worth covering is that a full battery charge simply holds less usable range than a full tank of gas. Because of this, fast chargers need to be spaced out more frequently than gas stations are if EVs are going to give drivers the same freedom of travel that gas powered cars currently do. The map here shows all gas station locations in San Diego alongside all public EV charging locations across California.
For EVs to become a true one to one replacement for gas cars, these issues need to be addressed, though the solution depends on which use case you're targeting. More charging stations need to be added in densely populated areas to support everyday driving, while Level 3 chargers specifically need to be placed frequently along long stretches of highway to allow for long distance EV travel. Additional Level 1 and 2 chargers could also be added at overnight motels to support road trips.
