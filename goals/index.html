Our Goals

By 2025, more than 1.25 million plug-in electric vehicles were registered across six Southern California counties — Los Angeles, Orange, San Diego, Riverside, San Bernardino, and Ventura — a weighted 7.80% share of the region's light-duty fleet. Adoption is uneven, and paradoxically so: it grows fastest where it is currently lowest. Orange County leads on share at 10.46% and San Bernardino sits lowest at 4.94% — yet San Bernardino (33.3% CAGR, 2018–2025) and Riverside (33.5%) are the fastest-growing counties, outpacing Los Angeles (25.8%) and Orange (25.1%). Together they form one of the largest, densest EV markets in the United States, and this report asks whether the public charging infrastructure serving them has kept pace.
The question is not abstract. As California moves toward its 2035 phase-out of new gasoline-vehicle sales under the Air Resources Board's Advanced Clean Cars II regulation (CARB, 2022), the ratio of vehicles to charging ports becomes a measurable indicator of whether the transition's infrastructure is ready. To evaluate it, this project benchmarks SoCal port deployment against the AB 2127 Second Assessment (CEC, 2024) — the state's standard, published in March 2024, estimating how many public Level 2 and DC-fast ports California needs to meet projected PEV demand.
Two clarifications matter at the outset. First, AB 2127 — a 2018 statute directing the CEC to assess statewide charging needs — frames adequacy in terms of light-duty public ports, both Level 2 and DC-fast, for the broader EV-using public rather than specific vehicle classes or corridors. Second, AB 2127 is not the federal NEVI program: NEVI funds corridor-specific DC-fast charging, while the AB 2127 assessment is a statewide port-count benchmark. The two are often referenced together but measure different things, and this report treats them as distinct.
The analysis is bounded by that frame: public Level 2 and DC-fast charging at the county level, 2018–2025. Because the Second Assessment is defined for light-duty plug-in electric vehicles, hydrogen refueling, medium- and heavy-duty charging, school bus charging, and private/home charging fall outside this scope and are excluded.
